01 - Housing Conditions

Southern Arizona housing conditions

Housing growth in metro Tucson is moving through a constrained pipeline: infill sites inside Tucson, master-planned growth in Marana and Sahuarita, redevelopment opportunities in older commercial corridors, and county approvals in unincorporated areas.

100yr

Water remains a development gate

Subdivisions in Arizona Active Management Areas generally need a 100-year assured water supply before final platting can move forward.

Supply

Growth is uneven by jurisdiction

Pima County grew from about 1.04M residents in 2020 to roughly 1.07M in 2025. Marana grew from 51,908 to 65,523 over the same period, while Tucson changed more slowly.

Cost

Suburban pipeline is expanding

Sahuarita grew from 34,134 residents in 2020 to about 37,694 in 2025. Marana and Sahuarita remain key areas for master-planned and edge-growth housing.

Governance

Approvals are still local

Mayor-and-council bodies, county supervisors, planning commissions, and development services departments control most zoning, plats, permits, and code changes.

02 - Authority Map

How housing decisions get approved

State law sets the outer rules. City and county decision makers adopt plans, zoning, fees, and project approvals. Staff administer permits and inspections.

01

Federal and tax-credit funding

HUD, CDBG, HOME, and Low-Income Housing Tax Credits fund restricted-affordability projects through federal, state, and local channels.

02

State law

The Arizona Legislature can preempt local zoning, development standards, accessory dwelling unit rules, and planning requirements.

03

General plans and zoning

Tucson, Pima County, Marana, Oro Valley, Sahuarita, and South Tucson adopt land-use plans and zoning maps.

04

Zoning and entitlements

Councils, supervisors, planning commissions, boards of adjustment, and development staff decide rezonings, variances, and site approvals.

05

Permits, infrastructure, and water

Building permits, impact fees, utility connections, roads, drainage, and 100-year assured water supply rules determine project readiness.

03 - Policy Initiatives

Current housing policy areas

Current policy points affecting zoning, permits, funding, and project delivery.

Density

Middle housing implementation

  • Arizona's 2024 middle-housing law requires larger cities to allow duplexes, triplexes, fourplexes, and townhomes in more residential areas.
  • Arizona's 2024 ADU law requires qualifying municipalities to allow accessory dwelling units on lots with single-family homes, with local code amendments carrying out the details.
Planning

General plan alignment

  • Tucson's Plan Tucson update and local general-plan cycles set land-use categories, growth areas, and infrastructure assumptions.
  • Arizona law requires voter ratification for municipal general plans in cities and towns, making plan updates a ballot-linked housing policy point.
Pipeline

Permitting and process reform

  • Arizona enacted residential zoning and permit-review legislation in 2024 to shorten local review timelines for some housing applications.
  • Tucson, Pima County, Marana, Oro Valley, Sahuarita, and South Tucson control local checklists, fee schedules, staff review cycles, and inspection capacity.
Capital Stack

Affordable housing gap funding

  • Low-Income Housing Tax Credits, HUD HOME/CDBG funds, city and county gap funding, land contributions, and vouchers can be layered into restricted-affordability projects.
  • Affordability periods, income limits, and rent restrictions are set through funding agreements and recorded covenants.
